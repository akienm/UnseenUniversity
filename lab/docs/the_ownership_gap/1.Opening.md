## Compiled Inference and the New Economics of AI Development

## Opening: The App That Keeps Getting More Expensive

A manager at a mid-sized company built a working application in a weekend using AI. He showed it to his director on Monday morning. It did exactly what he said it would do.

Six months later, the application costs more to run than a human developer would have charged to build it — and that number is climbing. Nobody knows why. Nobody knows how to fix it. The manager who built it in a weekend has moved on to the next thing.

This is not a story about AI failing. The application works. The manager was right about what he built.

The question nobody asked on Monday morning was: *what does it cost to own this?*

---

## Creation Cost vs. Ownership Cost

AI collapsed the cost of creation. A working prototype, a serviceable integration, a functional internal tool — these are now hours of work instead of weeks. That is real. The managers demonstrating weekend apps are not wrong about what they did.

What AI did not collapse is the cost of ownership: every bug found in production, every flaky test, every inference call that reinvents a wheel it invented last week, every dollar spent asking the AI to figure out something it already figured out — and forgot. These costs are recurring. They compound. They grow.

The two curves diverge fastest when there is no structure constraining the AI — no compiled patterns, no shared architecture, no one accountable for whether the system gets cheaper or more expensive to maintain over time.

That divergence is what organizations are discovering right now, as AI provider rates rise and production bills arrive. The media narrative is catching up: *AI is starting to cost more than humans.* The story is true, but it is misdiagnosed. The problem is not AI. The problem is unstructured AI at production scale.

## The Root Cause

Every time an AI system solves the same problem from scratch, it introduces variance and cost. A question answered once costs tokens. The same question answered a thousand times costs a thousand times as many tokens — unless someone captured the answer.

The organizations paying the most for AI are the ones where nothing gets captured. Every session starts fresh. Every pattern gets reinvented. Every deployment is a prototype that happens to have made it to production.

The organizations that will win are the ones that treat AI like what it actually is: a reasoning engine that can be progressively replaced by the outputs of its own reasoning. You use the AI to discover the pattern. Then you compile the pattern into something cheaper — a skill, a script, an infrastructure primitive. The AI does less figuring-out every week, and the system gets faster and cheaper as a result.

This is not a new idea. It is what software has always been. Hardware is compiled reasoning. A book is compiled reasoning. A standard operating procedure is compiled reasoning. The AI-specific insight is that you can use AI itself to discover and validate patterns before compiling them — collapsing the time from "someone figured this out" to "the organization runs it automatically."

We call this **compiled inference**.

## Two Ways to Build with AI

There are two ways to build something with AI, and they have very different trajectories.

The first way: put the whole problem in front of the most capable model you have, get a result, ship it. Fast. Impressive on Monday morning. Six months later, when something breaks, you go back to the same model with the same blank canvas and ask it to figure out what's wrong. It does — at the same cost as the first time, because nothing was captured. The reasoning evaporated the moment the session ended. Maintenance is just creation, over and over, forever.

The second way: break the problem into pieces. Start with the smallest, cheapest model that can handle each piece — and only escalate to a more capable model when the simpler one isn't enough. Each piece gets encoded as something durable: a skill, a script, a logged decision, a verifiable test. The expensive reasoning happens once, in pieces, and leaves something behind. The result — the working application — is almost incidental. It is what you get when you run the final test. The primary output is the structure: the compiled patterns, the logs, the chain of reasoning you can read and modify and hand to a junior model next time.

The first approach gets more expensive every month. The reasoning evaporates; nothing compounds. The second approach gets cheaper. Each week, more of the work runs at lower cost, with higher reliability, under lighter supervision.

This book is about the second approach.

## What This Book Is

This book is a practitioner's guide to building AI systems that get cheaper and more reliable over time instead of more expensive and more fragile.

It is built on several years of direct experience building a large AI reasoning system — one that has generated roughly 200,000 lines of working code, almost none of which has been reviewed by a human, designed entirely by the author. That system is not a research experiment. It runs. It improves itself. It costs less to operate each month than the month before.

The chapters that follow cover the full stack of that approach:

- **Compiled Inference** — the concept, the economics, and why the creation/ownership gap is the most important thing to understand about AI right now
- **Levers** — the keystone patterns that make the difference between AI that compounds in value and AI that compounds in cost
- **Workflow, Audits, and Model Selection** — the daily practice; how to structure work so that AI is always operating inside a constrained, verified space rather than a blank canvas
- **Skills as Built** — the actual implementation: what the skills look like, why they are shaped the way they are, and how they integrate into a self-improving system
- **The Platform** — the infrastructure substrate; why it is built the way it is
- **What We Built On It** — the system itself as proof of concept

The brief version of the argument is this: unstructured AI use gets more expensive over time. Structured AI use gets cheaper. The difference between those two trajectories is a set of practices and tools that most organizations do not yet have — and a function that knows how to build and maintain them.

That function already exists in most organizations. It is just not yet pointed at this problem.
